World & Historical Pies

Pastry wrapped around a filling is one of the most independently reinvented ideas in cooking — nearly every culture that has flour and an oven (or a tandoor, or a frying pan) has arrived at some version of it. This page rounds out the atlas with pies and pie-adjacent pastries from thirty more countries and regions across Europe, Canada, Asia, and Latin America. It isn't literally every nation on Earth — some places simply don't have a strong native pie tradition — but it's a genuine attempt at the map. (Portugal, Finland, Russia, Italy, Greece, France, England, Argentina, Jamaica, and Chile already have entries on the Regional and Savory pages; this page picks up where those leave off.)

9 recipes

German onion pie, Austrian lattice tart, and the family of paper-thin pastries — börek, khachapuri, byorek — that spread from Central Asia across the Ottoman world and the Caucasus.

Zwiebelkuchen (German Onion Pie)

Savory · Swabia, Germany

A yeasted-crust onion pie from southern Germany, thick with slow-caramelized onions, bacon, and caraway in a creamy custard — traditionally eaten in autumn alongside Federweisser, the season's first cloudy young wine.

Serves 8Prep 40 minBake 55 min

Ingredients

  • 2¼ cups flour
  • 1 packet yeast
  • ⅔ cup warm milk
  • 3 lb onions, thinly sliced
  • 6 oz bacon, diced
  • 1 tsp caraway seeds
  • 3 eggs
  • ¾ cup sour cream

Method

  1. Make a soft yeast dough with flour, yeast, and milk; let rise 45 minutes, then press into a deep tart pan.
  2. Cook bacon until crisp, add onions and caraway, and cook low and slow until deeply softened, about 30 minutes. Cool slightly.
  3. Whisk eggs and sour cream, then stir into the onion mixture; spread over the dough.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 50–55 minutes until the top is golden and the center is set.

Linzertorte

Sweet · Linz, Austria

A crumbly, spiced hazelnut pastry lattice over redcurrant or raspberry jam — often called the oldest cake still known by its original name, with a documented recipe from 1653 and another from 1696.

Serves 10Prep 35 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 1¾ cups flour
  • 1¼ cups ground hazelnuts
  • ¾ cup butter, cold
  • ⅔ cup sugar
  • 2 egg yolks
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • 1 lemon, zested
  • 1 cup redcurrant or raspberry jam

Method

  1. Combine flour, ground hazelnuts, cold butter, sugar, egg yolks, cinnamon, and lemon zest into a short, crumbly dough; chill 30 minutes.
  2. Press two-thirds of the dough into a tart pan; spread jam over the top.
  3. Roll the remaining dough into strips and weave a lattice over the jam.
  4. Bake at 350°F (175°C) for 30–35 minutes until golden. Cool fully before slicing — it's traditionally eaten a day later, once the flavors settle.

Historical note: A 2005 discovery in an Admont Abbey archive pushed the Linzertorte's earliest known recipe back to 1653, making it one of the oldest cakes on record still made from a recognizable version of its original recipe.

Irish Apple Amber Pie

Sweet · Ireland

A single-crust Irish classic: grated stewed apple sharpened with lemon, baked into a soft custard-like layer, then crowned with a tall, softly-peaked meringue — "amber" for the color the apple turns as it cooks down.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 6 cooking apples, peeled and grated
  • 1 lemon, zested and juiced
  • ⅔ cup sugar (filling)
  • 3 egg yolks (reserve whites)
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 3 egg whites (reserved)
  • ½ cup sugar, for meringue
  • 1 single pie crust, blind-baked

Method

  1. Cook grated apple with lemon zest and juice and sugar until soft and thick, about 10 minutes.
  2. Off heat, beat in egg yolks and butter; pour into the baked crust.
  3. Beat egg whites to soft peaks, gradually adding sugar until glossy and stiff.
  4. Pile the meringue over the apple layer and bake at 325°F (165°C) for 20–25 minutes until golden.

Welsh Leek & Cheese Pie

Savory · Wales

Leeks are Wales's national emblem, said to date to a battlefield custom of wearing one to tell allies apart — and this pie pairs them with sharp Welsh cheddar in a filling that leans on two of the country's proudest ingredients.

Serves 8Prep 25 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 4 leeks, sliced
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • 3 eggs
  • 1 cup cream
  • 1½ cups shredded sharp Welsh cheddar
  • 1 single pie crust, blind-baked

Method

  1. Sweat the leeks gently in butter until soft, about 12 minutes; cool slightly.
  2. Whisk eggs and cream, then stir in the leeks and most of the cheese.
  3. Pour into the blind-baked crust and scatter the remaining cheese on top.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 30–35 minutes until set and golden.

Su Böreği (Turkish "Water Börek")

Savory · Turkey

Layers of yufka dough boiled briefly before baking — the "water" in the name — giving this börek a softer, more lasagna-like texture than its crisp phyllo cousins. Filled with cheese and parsley, it anchors the vast Turkish börek family that spread with Turkic peoples from Central Asia across the Ottoman world.

Serves 10Prep 45 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 1 package yufka or phyllo sheets
  • 3 eggs
  • 1 cup milk
  • ½ cup olive oil
  • 14 oz feta or Turkish white cheese, crumbled
  • ½ cup parsley, chopped

Method

  1. Boil each sheet of dough briefly in salted water, then lay flat to cool.
  2. Whisk eggs, milk, and olive oil together.
  3. Layer half the boiled sheets in a baking dish, brushing each with the egg mixture; scatter with cheese and parsley.
  4. Layer the remaining sheets on top, brushing each, then pour any remaining egg mixture over the top.
  5. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 30–35 minutes until golden.

Adjaruli Khachapuri

Savory · Adjara, Georgia

A boat-shaped, open-topped bread pie from Georgia's Black Sea coast, filled with molten Imeretian and sulguni cheese and finished with a raw egg yolk and a pat of butter, stirred together tableside just before eating.

Makes 4Prep 40 min + riseBake 15 min

Ingredients

  • 4 cups flour
  • 1 packet yeast
  • 1¼ cups warm water
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 3 cups shredded mozzarella & feta, mixed
  • 4 egg yolks
  • 4 tbsp butter

Method

  1. Make a soft yeast dough with flour, yeast, water, and olive oil; let rise 1 hour.
  2. Divide into 4; roll each into an oval and pinch the long edges up into a boat shape, twisting the pointed ends.
  3. Fill each boat with cheese and bake at 475°F (245°C) for 10–12 minutes until the crust is golden.
  4. Pull out, crack a raw egg yolk into the center of each, add a pat of butter, and return to the oven just 2–3 minutes to warm the yolk without fully cooking it.
  5. Serve immediately; stir the yolk and butter into the cheese at the table.

Bosnian Burek

Savory · Balkans (Bosnia & Serbia)

Spiral-coiled phyllo filled with spiced ground beef, a Balkan staple that traces back to Ottoman-era börek. Serbia's version was reputedly brought to the city of Niš by an Istanbul baker in 1498, and the dish spread across former Yugoslavia from there.

Serves 8Prep 40 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground beef
  • 1 onion, grated
  • 1 tsp paprika
  • 1 package phyllo dough
  • ½ cup vegetable oil or melted butter
  • ¼ cup sparkling water, to brush

Method

  1. Mix ground beef with onion, paprika, salt, and pepper.
  2. Brush a phyllo sheet with oil, spread a thin line of filling along one edge, and roll into a long rope; coil it into a spiral in a round baking pan.
  3. Repeat with remaining sheets, coiling each rope to extend the spiral outward.
  4. Brush the top with oil and sparkling water (for extra crispness) and bake at 400°F (200°C) for 35–40 minutes until deep golden.

Armenian Spinach Byorek

Savory · Armenia

Triangular hand pies of phyllo folded around spinach, onion, and a blend of Armenian cheeses like lori or chechil, often lifted with a splash of anise-flavored raki — a close cousin of Greek spanakopita, but with its own herb and cheese profile.

Makes 16Prep 35 minBake 25 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb spinach, wilted and squeezed dry
  • 8 oz Armenian string cheese or feta, shredded
  • 1 onion, finely chopped
  • 1 tbsp tarragon, chopped
  • 1 tsp raki or anise liqueur (optional)
  • 8 phyllo sheets, halved lengthwise
  • ½ cup butter, melted

Method

  1. Mix spinach, cheese, onion, tarragon, and raki, if using.
  2. Brush a phyllo strip with butter, place a spoonful of filling at one end, and fold flag-style into a triangle.
  3. Repeat with remaining strips and filling; brush the tops with butter.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 20–25 minutes until golden and crisp.

Limburgse Vlaai

Sweet · Limburg, Netherlands

A wide, shallow, yeast-doughed fruit pie from the southern Dutch province of Limburg — legend holds that in 1189, townsfolk of Sint-Truiden ended a siege by presenting the attacking duke with a pastry called plăcintă, baked to an old local recipe. As of 2024, "Limburgse vlaai" is a protected EU name, and only pies baked whole, filling and all, within Limburg may use it.

Serves 12Prep 30 min + riseBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 3 cups flour
  • 1 packet yeast
  • ¾ cup warm milk
  • ¼ cup sugar
  • 1 egg
  • 4 tbsp butter, softened
  • 4 cups pitted cherries or sliced apricots
  • 3 tbsp cornstarch, ⅓ cup sugar (filling)

Method

  1. Make a soft yeast dough with flour, yeast, milk, sugar, egg, and butter; let rise 45 minutes.
  2. Press the dough into a wide, shallow tart pan, building a slight rim.
  3. Toss the fruit with cornstarch and sugar; spread over the dough.
  4. Weave leftover dough scraps into a thin lattice over the top, if desired, and bake at 400°F (200°C) for 25–30 minutes until golden.

See also: Pennsylvania Dutch settlers carried the vlaai tradition to America, where "vlaai" is thought to have blurred into "shoofly" — see the Shoofly Pie on the Sweet page.

Ontario Butter Tart

Sweet · Ontario, Canada

A small, gooey tart of butter, brown sugar, and egg — one of the few dishes considered genuinely and uniquely Canadian in origin, with raisin-vs-plain and runny-vs-firm-filling debates that run as deep as any regional food rivalry.

Makes 12Prep 25 minBake 18 min

Ingredients

  • 2 single pie crusts, cut into 12 rounds
  • ½ cup butter, melted
  • 1 cup brown sugar
  • 2 eggs
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 tbsp vinegar
  • ⅓ cup raisins (optional)

Method

  1. Press the dough rounds into a muffin tin.
  2. Whisk melted butter, brown sugar, eggs, vanilla, and vinegar until smooth.
  3. Drop a few raisins into each shell if using, then fill two-thirds full with the butter mixture.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 15–18 minutes until the pastry is golden and the filling is bubbling but not fully set in the center.
  5. Cool in the tin 10 minutes before removing.

Saskatoon Berry Pie

Sweet · Saskatchewan & the Prairies

The berry that gave the city of Saskatoon its name — related to the apple and pear rather than the blueberry it resembles, with an almondy sweetness First Nations peoples once pounded into pemmican with dried bison.

Serves 8Prep 25 minBake 50 min

Ingredients

  • 5 cups saskatoon berries (or blueberries)
  • ¾ cup sugar
  • ¼ cup flour
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Toss berries with sugar, flour, and lemon juice.
  2. Fill a crust-lined dish and top with lattice.
  3. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 20 minutes, then reduce to 375°F (190°C) for 25–30 minutes more.
  4. Cool at least 2 hours; serve with vanilla ice cream, whipped cream, or a slice of cheddar.

Fact check: Saskatoon berry pie appeared on a Canada Post "Sweet Canada" stamp in 2019, representing the Prairie provinces among five iconic Canadian desserts.

Flapper Pie

Sweet · The Prairies, Canada

A graham-crumb crust, vanilla custard, and meringue — the "forgotten Prairie pie," popular across Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba since the 1920s, when it picked up its flapper-era name.

Serves 8Prep 25 minBake 15 min

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups graham cracker crumbs
  • 6 tbsp butter, melted
  • ⅔ cup sugar (custard)
  • ¼ cup cornstarch
  • 2½ cups milk
  • 3 egg yolks (reserve whites)
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 3 egg whites + ½ cup sugar (meringue)

Method

  1. Mix graham crumbs with melted butter; press into a pie dish, reserving 2 tbsp for topping.
  2. Whisk sugar, cornstarch, milk, and egg yolks; cook over medium heat, stirring, until thick. Stir in vanilla and pour into the crust.
  3. Beat egg whites with sugar to stiff peaks; spread over the hot custard, sealing to the edges.
  4. Sprinkle with the reserved crumbs and bake at 350°F (175°C) for 12–15 minutes until the meringue is golden. Chill before slicing.

Newfoundland Bakeapple (Cloudberry) Pie

Sweet · Newfoundland & Labrador

Despite the name, no apples involved — "bakeapple" is the Newfoundland name for the wild cloudberry, a seedy, honey-colored bog fruit said to scent the boggy barrens where it grows with a smell like apple pie.

Serves 8Prep 20 minBake 45 min

Ingredients

  • 4 cups bakeapples (cloudberries)
  • ¾ cup sugar
  • 3 tbsp flour
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Toss cloudberries with sugar and flour.
  2. Fill a crust-lined dish and top with lattice strips.
  3. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 40–45 minutes until the crust is golden and the filling bubbles.
  4. Cool at least 2 hours before slicing.

Tarte au Sucre (Quebec Sugar Pie)

Sweet · Quebec, Canada

The quintessential Québécois winter dessert: brown sugar and cream cooked down until they nearly set into a single, barely-sliceable, deeply caramel filling in a butter crust.

Serves 8Prep 15 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 2 cups brown sugar
  • 3 tbsp flour
  • 1 cup heavy cream
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 single pie crust

Method

  1. Whisk brown sugar and flour, then whisk in cream.
  2. Cook over medium heat, stirring, until thick and glossy; stir in butter and vanilla.
  3. Pour into an unbaked crust.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 30–35 minutes until the top is set with a slight jiggle. Cool completely — it firms considerably as it cools.

Nanaimo Freezer Pie

Sweet · Nanaimo, British Columbia

A no-bake pie built from the three layers of a classic Nanaimo bar — a chocolate-coconut base, a custard-flavored buttercream middle, and a chocolate ganache top — reshaped into a sliceable frozen pie.

Serves 10Prep 30 minFreeze 4 hr

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups graham crumbs
  • ½ cup shredded coconut
  • ¼ cup cocoa powder
  • ½ cup butter, melted (base)
  • ½ cup butter, softened (filling)
  • 2 cups powdered sugar
  • 3 tbsp custard powder or instant vanilla pudding mix
  • 4 oz chocolate, melted, for the top

Method

  1. Mix graham crumbs, coconut, cocoa, and melted butter; press into a pie dish and chill.
  2. Beat softened butter with powdered sugar and custard powder until fluffy; spread over the base.
  3. Pour melted chocolate over the top in an even layer.
  4. Freeze at least 4 hours. Let sit 10 minutes at room temperature before slicing.

Cipaille (Six-Pâtes)

Savory · Quebec, Canada — historical

A towering, multi-layered logging-camp pie — alternating layers of pastry and game meat (traditionally moose, hare, partridge, and pork) stacked several courses deep in a deep pot, with a hole in the top crust acting as a chimney so steam can escape during the long, slow bake.

Serves 10Prep 1 hrBake 4 hr

Ingredients

  • 1 lb each cubed pork, beef, and chicken (or game)
  • 4 potatoes, cubed
  • 2 onions, sliced
  • 2 tsp mixed savory spice (allspice, cloves, pepper)
  • 3 double pie crusts
  • 2 cups stock

Method

  1. Line a deep, oven-safe pot with a layer of crust.
  2. Layer meat, potato, onion, and spice, then a sheet of crust; repeat to build three or more layers, finishing with a top crust.
  3. Cut a small hole in the center of the top crust and pour stock in gradually as it bakes to keep the layers moist.
  4. Bake at 250°F (120°C) for 4–5 hours, covering loosely with foil if the top browns too fast, until the meat is fork-tender.

Historical note: Also called cipâte or "sea pie," cipaille was built to feed logging and fishing crews for days — a single pot could be reheated meal after meal.

Pâté Chinois

Savory · Quebec, Canada

Ground beef, canned corn, and mashed potato layered like a shepherd's pie, with no pastry crust at all. Quebec folklore credits Chinese railway camp cooks with inventing an economical version of British cottage pie to feed 19th-century rail workers — a story food historians treat with some healthy skepticism, but one that's stuck to the dish's name regardless.

Serves 8Prep 25 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 1½ lb ground beef
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 2 cans (15 oz) creamed corn
  • 2 lb potatoes, boiled and mashed
  • 4 tbsp butter
  • ¼ cup milk

Method

  1. Brown beef with onion; season and spread in a baking dish.
  2. Layer the creamed corn over the beef.
  3. Mash potatoes with butter and milk until smooth, then spread over the corn layer.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 25–30 minutes until golden in spots on top.

Asia

7 recipes

From a 13th-century Delhi Sultanate court pastry to a coconut pie invented in a Philippine bakery in 1965 — plus the Portuguese-to-Guangzhou-to-Hong Kong custard tart that may be the most well-traveled pastry on this whole site.

Mughal-Era Sanbusak

Savory · Delhi Sultanate, from the 1300s

The historical ancestor of the modern samosa: a small pastry stuffed with minced meat, almonds, pistachios, and spice, recorded at the royal court of the Delhi Sultanate. The poet Amir Khusrau described nobles enjoying "samosa prepared from meat, ghee, onion" around 1300 CE, and traveler Ibn Battuta wrote of one served before a state banquet's third course.

Makes 16Prep 40 minFry 3 min each

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground lamb or beef
  • 1 onion, minced
  • ¼ cup almonds & pistachios, chopped
  • 1 tsp garam masala
  • ½ tsp ground ginger
  • 16 spring roll or samosa wrappers
  • 3 cups oil, for frying

Method

  1. Cook meat and onion until browned; stir in nuts, garam masala, and ginger, then cool.
  2. Fold each wrapper into a cone, fill, and seal the edge with a flour-water paste.
  3. Fry in 350°F (175°C) oil, turning, until golden and crisp, about 3 minutes.
  4. Drain and serve hot.

Historical note: The word "samosa" traces to the Middle Persian sambosag. The dish reached Delhi via Central Asian and Middle Eastern chefs working in the royal kitchens in the 13th–14th centuries, and is documented again in the 16th-century Mughal court text the Ain-i-Akbari.

Uzbek Somsa

Savory · Uzbekistan & Central Asia

A flaky, tandoor-baked pastry stuffed with lamb, onion, and cumin — related to the samosa by name and a shared Silk Road history, but baked rather than fried, traditionally stuck to the inner wall of a clay tandoor oven until golden.

Makes 12Prep 40 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground lamb, with some fat
  • 2 onions, finely chopped
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • 2½ cups flour
  • ¾ cup warm water
  • ½ cup ghee or melted butter
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • Sesame seeds, to finish

Method

  1. Mix lamb, onion, cumin, salt, and pepper by hand until well combined.
  2. Knead flour and water into a soft dough; rest 20 minutes, then roll thin and brush with ghee, folding into layers.
  3. Divide the dough, wrap each piece around a portion of filling, and pinch closed into a square or triangle.
  4. Brush with egg, sprinkle with sesame seeds, and bake at 400°F (200°C) for 25–30 minutes until deep golden and flaky.

Tibetan Shabhaley (Sha Balep)

Savory · Tibet

A pan-fried meat-filled bread from Tibet's high plateau, made with a simple flour-and-water dough wrapped around spiced ground meat and greens, then shallow-fried until the crust turns crisp and blistered.

Makes 10Prep 35 minFry 6 min each

Ingredients

  • 2 cups flour
  • ¾ cup cold water
  • ½ lb ground beef or yak
  • 1 cup cabbage or spinach, finely chopped
  • 2 scallions, chopped
  • 1 tsp ginger, grated
  • 3 tbsp oil, for frying

Method

  1. Knead flour and water into a smooth dough; rest 20 minutes.
  2. Mix meat with cabbage, scallions, and ginger.
  3. Roll dough into circles, fill with meat mixture, and fold in half, sealing the edge.
  4. Pan-fry in oil over medium heat, 3 minutes per side, until golden and cooked through.

Xian Bing (Chinese Meat Pie)

Savory · Northern China

A pan-fried stuffed flatbread from northern China, its thin dough wrapped around juicy ground beef and scallion, then griddled until the crust turns crisp and lightly charred in spots — closer to a large dumpling than a Western pie, but built on the same logic of pastry wrapped around a filling.

Makes 8Prep 40 minFry 5 min each

Ingredients

  • 2½ cups flour
  • 1 cup hot water
  • 1 lb ground beef
  • 3 scallions, chopped
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tsp sesame oil
  • 3 tbsp oil, for frying

Method

  1. Gradually mix hot water into flour to form a dough; knead until smooth, then rest 20 minutes.
  2. Mix beef with scallions, soy sauce, and sesame oil.
  3. Divide dough into 8; roll each thin, place filling in the center, and gather the edges up to seal, then flatten gently.
  4. Pan-fry in oil over medium heat, 2–3 minutes per side, until golden and cooked through.

Hong Kong Egg Tart (Dan Tat)

Sweet · Hong Kong & Guangzhou

A flaky-crusted custard tart with a genuinely global family tree: inspired by English custard tarts, first sold in Guangzhou department stores around 1927, and reintroduced in a Portuguese-style version by a British pharmacist's Macau bakery in 1989 — now a dim sum and bakery staple across the region.

Makes 12Prep 30 minBake 18 min

Ingredients

  • 1 single pie crust or puff pastry, cut into 12 rounds
  • ½ cup sugar
  • ⅔ cup hot water
  • 4 eggs
  • ⅓ cup evaporated milk
  • ½ tsp vanilla

Method

  1. Press pastry rounds into a muffin tin, going slightly up the sides.
  2. Dissolve sugar in hot water and let cool.
  3. Whisk eggs, evaporated milk, sugar water, and vanilla; strain for a silky texture.
  4. Fill each shell three-quarters full and bake at 400°F (200°C) for 15–18 minutes until the custard is just set with a slight jiggle. Cool before removing from the tin.

Bánh Pâté Chaud

Savory · Vietnam

A flaky puff-pastry meat pie born from French colonial Vietnam, its name simply French for "hot pastry." Vietnamese bakers built their own version around ground pork, wood ear mushroom, and fish sauce — now a breakfast and bánh mì-shop staple across Vietnam and its diaspora.

Makes 12Prep 35 minBake 25 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground pork
  • ¼ cup dried wood ear mushroom, soaked and chopped
  • 1 shallot, minced
  • 1 tbsp fish sauce
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • 2 sheets puff pastry
  • 1 egg, beaten

Method

  1. Mix pork with mushroom, shallot, fish sauce, sugar, and pepper.
  2. Cut puff pastry into 24 small rounds. Spoon filling onto 12, top with the remaining rounds, and crimp the edges.
  3. Brush with beaten egg.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 20–25 minutes until puffed and golden.

Filipino Buko Pie

Sweet · Laguna, Philippines

Young coconut in a sweet condensed-milk custard, invented in 1965 by Nanette Pahud, who wanted to open a bakery but couldn't easily get apples in Laguna — so she substituted the region's abundant young coconuts and created what's now the Philippines' most iconic pie.

Serves 8Prep 25 minBake 45 min

Ingredients

  • 3 cups young coconut meat, shredded
  • 1 can (14 oz) sweetened condensed milk
  • 1 cup young coconut water
  • 3 tbsp cornstarch
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Whisk condensed milk, coconut water, and cornstarch together; fold in the shredded coconut.
  2. Fill a crust-lined dish and top with a second crust; seal and vent.
  3. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 40–45 minutes until golden.
  4. Cool before slicing; best served the day it's baked.

Historical note: Pahud's bakery, Orient — The Original Buko Pie Bakeshop, is still run by her family in Los Baños, Laguna, now widely considered the "buko pie capital" of the Philippines.

Latin America

6 recipes

A brothy Bolivian breakfast pastry, a Brazilian baked pie, and a Puerto Rican plantain "lasagna" — six more stops on the map. (Argentine beef empanadas, Jamaican patties, and Chilean pastel de choclo already have full recipes on the Savory page.)

Bolivian Salteña

Savory · Bolivia

Bolivia's national pastry: a slightly sweet, empanada-like dough sealed around a juicy, gelatin-set stew of beef, potato, peas, and a touch of chili, which turns brothy again as the pie bakes. Eaten upright, in small bites from the top, so the stew doesn't spill. The name honors Juana Manuela Gorriti, a 19th-century writer from Salta, Argentina, who is said to have invented the dish while living in exile in Bolivia.

Makes 10Prep 1 hr + chillBake 25 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb beef, diced small
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 1 cup beef stock
  • 1 tbsp unflavored gelatin
  • 1 potato, diced
  • ½ cup peas
  • 1 tbsp ají amarillo paste (or paprika + cayenne)
  • 1 hard-boiled egg, chopped
  • 3 cups flour, ⅓ cup sugar, ½ cup lard (dough)

Method

  1. Cook beef, onion, potato, and ají paste with stock until the potato is tender; season well.
  2. Dissolve gelatin in a little warm water and stir into the hot filling; stir in peas and egg. Chill until firm, at least 4 hours (this jellied stew is the "juice" inside the baked pie).
  3. Make a slightly sweet dough from flour, sugar, lard, and warm water; rest 20 minutes.
  4. Roll into circles, spoon in a portion of the cold, firm filling, fold, and twist-seal the edge into a rope pattern.
  5. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 20–25 minutes until golden. Cool slightly — the filling will be very hot and liquid straight from the oven.

Empadão (Brazilian Chicken Pie)

Savory · Brazil

A deep, double-crusted pie of shredded chicken, hearts of palm, olives, and tomato — the full-size relative of Brazil's tiny individual empadinhas, sized for a family table instead of a single hand.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 3 cups cooked chicken, shredded
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 2 tomatoes, diced
  • ½ cup hearts of palm, sliced
  • ¼ cup green olives, sliced
  • 3 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 double pie crusts
  • 1 egg, beaten

Method

  1. Cook onion and tomato until soft; stir in tomato paste, then fold in chicken, hearts of palm, and olives. Season and cool.
  2. Fill a crust-lined deep dish, top with a second crust, seal, and vent.
  3. Brush with beaten egg.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 35–40 minutes until deep golden.

Pastelón (Puerto Rican Plantain Pie)

Savory · Puerto Rico

Sometimes called "Puerto Rican lasagna": layers of sweet, caramelized ripe plantain standing in for pasta, sandwiching a seasoned beef picadillo and melted cheese — no wheat pastry crust at all, but built and sliced exactly like a pie.

Serves 8Prep 40 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 4 very ripe (black-skinned) plantains, sliced lengthwise
  • 3 tbsp oil, for frying
  • 1 lb ground beef
  • ½ cup tomato sauce
  • ¼ cup sofrito (onion, pepper, garlic, cilantro)
  • 2 eggs, beaten
  • 1½ cups shredded mozzarella

Method

  1. Fry the plantain slices in oil until soft and caramelized; drain on paper towels.
  2. Cook beef with sofrito and tomato sauce until well seasoned.
  3. Layer plantains in a baking dish, then beef, then cheese; repeat, finishing with plantain and cheese.
  4. Pour beaten egg evenly over the top.
  5. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 30–35 minutes until set and golden. Rest 10 minutes before slicing.

Pay de Limón

Sweet · Mexico

A Mexican household staple built on the same condensed-milk logic as Key Lime Pie, but made with everyday limón and often set into a crust of crushed Marie cookies instead of graham crackers — usually topped with a plain layer of whipped cream rather than meringue.

Serves 8Prep 15 minChill 3 hr

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups crushed Marie or graham cookies
  • 6 tbsp butter, melted
  • 1 can (14 oz) sweetened condensed milk
  • 1 cup Mexican crema or sour cream
  • ½ cup lime juice
  • 1 cup whipped cream, to finish

Method

  1. Mix cookie crumbs and melted butter; press into a pie dish and chill.
  2. Whisk condensed milk, crema, and lime juice until thick and smooth.
  3. Pour into the crust and chill at least 3 hours until set.
  4. Top with whipped cream just before serving.

Pastelitos de Guayaba

Sweet · Cuba

Flaky puff-pastry hand pies filled with sweet guava paste and, in the most popular version, a slice of cream cheese to cut the sweetness — a fixture of Cuban and Cuban-American bakery cases, classically eaten alongside a strong coffee.

Makes 12Prep 20 minBake 20 min

Ingredients

  • 2 sheets puff pastry
  • 8 oz guava paste, sliced
  • 4 oz cream cheese, sliced (optional)
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 2 tbsp coarse sugar, to finish

Method

  1. Cut puff pastry into 24 small squares.
  2. Place a slice of guava paste (and cream cheese, if using) on 12 squares, leaving a border.
  3. Top with the remaining squares and crimp the edges with a fork.
  4. Brush with beaten egg and sprinkle with coarse sugar.
  5. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 18–20 minutes until puffed and golden.

Empanadas de Viento

Sweet-savory · Ecuador

"Wind empanadas" — named for how dramatically they puff up in the fryer. A simple wheat dough wrapped around melting mozzarella, fried until blistered and golden, then dusted with sugar while still hot: a beloved Andean breakfast street food that walks the sweet-savory line in a single bite.

Makes 10Prep 25 minFry 3 min each

Ingredients

  • 2½ cups flour
  • 2 tbsp butter, melted
  • ¾ cup warm water
  • ½ tsp salt
  • 2 cups shredded mozzarella
  • 3 cups oil, for frying
  • Granulated sugar, to finish

Method

  1. Mix flour, melted butter, warm water, and salt into a smooth dough; rest 15 minutes.
  2. Roll into thin circles, add a spoonful of cheese to each, fold in half, and seal the edges well.
  3. Fry in 350°F (175°C) oil until puffed and golden, about 3 minutes, turning once.
  4. Drain briefly, then dust generously with sugar while still hot.
