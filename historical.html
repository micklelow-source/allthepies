Section one

Historical Pies

Pie didn't start as dessert — it started as a storage container. Follow the crust from Roman honey cakes to Depression-era water pie, then bake eighteen recipes recreated from the historical record, sorted into historical apple pies, rare & forgotten pies, and Depression-era desperation pies.

Historical Apple Pies Rare & Forgotten Depression-Era
c. 9500–2000 BCE · Ancient Egypt

The first pastry-wrapped foods

Flat, round flour cakes filled with honey and dates appear in the archaeological record as far back as the Egyptian Neolithic, and honeyed pastries were found among goods left in the tomb of Pharaoh Ramesses II. It isn't "pie" as we'd recognize it, but it's the first evidence of dough deliberately used to hold a filling.

c. 1st century BCE · Greece & Rome

Greek pastry, Roman placenta

Greek bakers are generally credited with the first true pastry shell — a simple water-and-flour paste wrapped around meat so it could cook over a fire without drying out. The Romans ran with the idea: their honeyed, rye-crusted goat cheese pie is the oldest pie recipe known to survive in writing, and their layered cheese-and-honey pastry, placenta, is a distant, denser ancestor of cheesecake.

1303 · Yorkshire, England

The word "pie" enters the record

The earliest known written use of the word "pie" turns up in the expense accounts of Bolton Priory. Its origin is still debated — one theory ties it to the magpie, a bird notorious for collecting odds and ends, much like the mixed, whatever-we-have fillings of the era.

14th–16th century · Medieval & Tudor England

The coffyn: pastry as Tupperware

Ovens were expensive and fuel was scarce, so most households cooked over an open fire — and pie was the workaround. A thick, deliberately inedible case of flour and water called a coffyn (simply the word for a box or basket) held a filling of beef, pigeon, magpie, or whatever meat was on hand while it cooked, and kept it sealed and preserved afterward. The 1390 cookbook The Forme of Cury, written for the court of Richard II, records fillings of minced pork, hard-boiled egg, and cheese, colored with saffron. Open-topped versions were called "traps." The crust usually wasn't eaten at all — it was cracked open like a container, and the hardened shell was sometimes reused to thicken stew.

15th–16th century · Royal courts

Pie as theater

Wealthy households turned pie into spectacle. Songbirds, peacocks (baked, then remounted in their own skin and feathers), and even live birds were baked into "surprise pies" for banquet entertainment — the origin of "four and twenty blackbirds baked in a pie." At the 1429 coronation feast of the boy-king Henry VI, pies were used to display the status of the kitchen as much as to feed the guests.

16th–17th century · Sugar arrives

From meat case to dessert

Sugar was a rare, costly status symbol in medieval Europe, so early fruit "tarts" were unsweetened. Once British colonization of the Caribbean made sugar (and the molasses that came with the rum trade) more affordable, sweet fruit pies became realistic for ordinary households. Lattice-top crusts appear around the 17th century, and mince pies — by then a mix of meat, dried fruit, and spice — became firmly associated with Christmas feasting.

1620s–1700s · Colonial America

Preservation, not pumpkin

English settlers brought their savory pie tradition with them, and pie became a practical way to stretch a harvest through winter. Contrary to the classroom version of the story, the 1621 harvest feast now remembered as "the first Thanksgiving" almost certainly did not include pumpkin pie — the earliest known pumpkin pie recipe appears in an English cookbook in 1675, and it didn't become a common American dish until the early 1800s.

1796 · The first American cookbook

Apple pie goes on the record

Amelia Simmons' American Cookery, the first cookbook published in the United States, includes two apple pie recipes. Apples kept well through winter when dried, which made apple pie an easy staple — one more reason the dish quietly became a symbol of American cooking despite being an English import.

1800s · Desperation pies

Pie as budget management

Nineteenth-century home cooks — usually women managing a household budget — got inventive when "real" fillings weren't affordable. Vinegar pie mimicked lemon filling using pantry staples. Sugar cream pie and Kentucky transparent pie used little more than cream, sugar, and flour. When apples ran out, "mock apple pie" was built from crackers, sugar, and cinnamon — a trick pioneers used on wagon trains and one that Ritz Crackers would later turn into a famous marketing recipe in the 1930s.

1930s · Regional identity

A pie for every pantry

As the country industrialized, pie fillings started to reflect local agriculture: cream and cheese pies in the dairy-rich Midwest, maple pies in the North (a sweetener Indigenous communities taught colonists to tap and boil), molasses pies where the rum trade had made it cheap, and — once corn syrup manufacturers started printing the recipe on the bottle in the 1930s — pecan pie across the South.

1975 & 1986 · Two pie holidays

National Pie Day and Pi Day

In 1975, Boulder, Colorado schoolteacher Charlie Papazian declared his own birthday, January 23, "National Pie Day" as a class project. The American Pie Council adopted and began promoting it in 1986. It's unrelated to Pi Day (March 14, or 3.14), a separate celebration of the mathematical constant that pie-shop marketing has happily borrowed ever since — round pies being the obvious pun.

Historical apple pies

6 recipes

The apple pie isn't one recipe but a family tree — English, Dutch, French, and colonial branches that all grew from the same 14th-century root. Here are six documented versions, oldest to newest. (The Elizabethan Spiced Apple Coffyn below is the earliest.)

Dutch Appeltaart

Sweet · Netherlands, from 1514

The deep, latticed Dutch apple pie whose recipes go back to a 1514 Brussels cookbook — the direct ancestor of the American version, built on a thick, crumbly bottom-and-side crust with cinnamon, lemon, and raisins.

Serves 10Prep 40 minBake 70 min

Ingredients

  • 2¾ cups flour
  • 1 cup cold butter, cubed
  • ¾ cup brown sugar
  • 1 egg
  • 7 tart apples, sliced
  • ½ cup raisins
  • 2 tsp cinnamon
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice

Method

  1. Rub flour, butter, half the sugar, and the egg into a dough; press two-thirds into the bottom and up the sides of a tall springform pan.
  2. Toss apples with raisins, cinnamon, remaining sugar, and lemon juice; pack tightly into the crust.
  3. Roll the remaining dough into strips and lay a wide lattice over the top.
  4. Bake at 350°F (175°C) for 65–70 minutes until deep gold. Cool fully before releasing the pan.

Historical note: A Dutch cookbook printed in Brussels in 1514, Een notabel boecxken van cokeryen, records a recipe for appeltaerten — among the oldest surviving apple pie recipes anywhere.

recreated from Dutch recipes dating to 1514

Tarte Tatin

Sweet · France, 1880s

The famous upside-down French apple tart: apples caramelized in butter and sugar in the pan, covered with pastry, baked, and flipped so the glossy caramelized fruit sits on top.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 6 firm apples, peeled, halved
  • ¾ cup sugar
  • 6 tbsp butter
  • 1 sheet puff pastry
  • Pinch salt

Method

  1. Melt butter and sugar in an oven-safe skillet over medium heat until it turns amber caramel.
  2. Arrange apple halves tightly, cut-side up, in the caramel; cook 15 minutes until they soften and color.
  3. Lay puff pastry over the top, tucking the edges down inside the pan.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 25–30 minutes until the pastry is puffed and golden.
  5. Cool 5 minutes, then invert onto a plate — carefully, as the caramel is hot.

Historical note: Named for the Tatin sisters, who ran a hotel in Lamotte-Beuvron; the upside-down method is said to have started as a happy kitchen accident in the 1880s.

recreated from the classic Sologne, France recipe

Cheshire Pork & Apple Pie

Savory-sweet · England, 1747

From Hannah Glasse's landmark 1747 cookbook: layers of pork loin and tart apple, lightly sweetened and moistened with white wine — a savory pie that shows how blurry the sweet/savory line once was.

Serves 6Prep 30 minBake 60 min

Ingredients

  • 1½ lb pork loin, thinly sliced
  • 4 tart apples, sliced
  • 2 tbsp sugar
  • ½ tsp nutmeg
  • ½ cup white wine
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • 2 double pie crusts
  • 1 tsp salt, ½ tsp pepper

Method

  1. Season pork with salt and pepper. In a crust-lined dish, layer pork and apple slices, sprinkling each layer with sugar and nutmeg.
  2. Dot with butter and pour in the white wine.
  3. Top with a second crust, seal, and cut a steam vent.
  4. Bake at 350°F (175°C) for 55–60 minutes until the pork is cooked through and the crust is deep gold.

Historical note: Hannah Glasse's The Art of Cookery Made Plain and Easy (1747) was one of the most influential English cookbooks of its century and crossed the Atlantic with colonists.

recreated from Hannah Glasse, 1747

Marlborough Pie

Sweet · England/New England, from 1660

A single-crust apple-custard pie flavored with lemon, nutmeg, and sherry — a New England Thanksgiving staple through the 1800s that nearly vanished by the 1920s. Grated apple gives it a texture between apple pie and pumpkin pie.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 2 tart apples, grated (about 1 cup)
  • ¾ cup sugar
  • 2 tbsp lemon juice, plus zest
  • 2 tbsp dry sherry
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • 3 eggs
  • ½ cup heavy cream
  • 1 tsp nutmeg
  • 1 single pie crust, blind-baked

Method

  1. Toss grated apple with sugar, lemon juice and zest, and sherry; sauté in butter 12–15 minutes until soft. Cool.
  2. Whisk eggs, cream, and nutmeg, then stir in the apple mixture.
  3. Pour into the blind-baked crust.
  4. Bake at 350°F (175°C) for 35–40 minutes until the custard is set but not browned. Serve warm or at room temperature.

Historical note: The first known apple-custard pie recipe appears in Robert May's The Accomplisht Cook (1660); Marlborough pudding is in Amelia Simmons' 1796 American Cookery. Its decline is sometimes tied to the temperance movement's suspicion of the sherry.

recreated from The Accomplisht Cook (1660) & American Cookery (1796)

Buttered Apple Pie

Sweet · America, 1796

One of the apple pies in Amelia Simmons' American Cookery (1796), the first cookbook published in the United States — stewed apples enriched with plenty of butter, rosewater, and warm spice, then re-baked in a fresh crust.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 6 apples, peeled and quartered
  • 6 tbsp butter
  • ½ cup sugar
  • 1 tbsp rosewater (optional)
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • ½ tsp mace or nutmeg
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Stew apples with a splash of water until just tender, then drain.
  2. Mash lightly with 4 tbsp butter, sugar, rosewater, cinnamon, and mace.
  3. Fill a crust-lined dish, dot with the remaining butter, and add a top crust; vent and crimp.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 35–40 minutes until golden.

Historical note: Simmons' book listed apple pie, buttered apple pie, and Marlborough pudding side by side — an early snapshot of how many apple pies Americans already kept in rotation by 1796.

recreated from American Cookery, 1796

Elizabethan Spiced Apple Coffyn

Sweet · England, 1545

Adapted from a 1545 recipe in "A Propre new booke of Cokery," one of the earliest known written apple pie recipes — saffron, cinnamon, ginger, and butter, before sugar was an everyday ingredient.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 45 min

Ingredients

  • 6 firm apples, peeled, cored, sliced
  • 4 tbsp butter
  • Pinch saffron threads
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • 1 tsp ground ginger
  • ⅓ cup sugar
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Warm butter with a little water and the saffron until golden and fragrant.
  2. Toss apple slices with the saffron butter, cinnamon, ginger, and sugar.
  3. Fill a crust-lined dish with the apples, dot with any remaining butter, and top with a lattice or full crust.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 40–45 minutes until the crust is deep gold and the apples are tender.

Historical note: The original instructions simply read "season your apples with cinnamon, ginger, and sugar enough" — a reminder that pie recipes were guidelines, not formulas, for most of history.

recreated from A Propre new booke of Cokery, 1545

Rare & forgotten pies

6 recipes

The genuinely obscure end of the pie world — medieval hand pies, an 18th-century wedding-feast chicken pie, and a Cornish pie that stares back at you. (The Medieval Coffyn Pye, Roman Honey & Cheese Pie, and Colonial Pumpkin Pudding below round out the six.)

Medieval Chewettes

Savory-sweet · England, 14th century

Small, muffin-sized "coffin" hand pies from the 1300s, filled with minced meat or offal, hard-boiled egg, dried fruit, and spice — a savory-sweet mixture that food historians see as a direct ancestor of the mince pie.

Makes 12Prep 35 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • ½ lb minced pork or chicken, cooked
  • 2 hard-boiled eggs, chopped
  • ⅓ cup currants or chopped dates
  • ½ tsp cinnamon
  • ½ tsp ginger
  • Pinch saffron
  • 2 double pie crusts
  • 1 egg, beaten

Method

  1. Mix cooked meat with chopped egg, currants, spices, and saffron.
  2. Line the cups of a muffin tin with dough circles (these are the small "coffins").
  3. Fill each, top with a dough lid, and seal; brush with beaten egg.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 25–30 minutes until golden. Serve warm or cold.

Historical note: "Chewettes" (also spelled chawettys or chewetts) appear across medieval European sources with a flavor profile close to modern mincemeat — sweet, spiced, and meaty all at once.

recreated from 14th-century English & Continental sources

Colonial Chicken Pie

Savory · America, 18th century

The thrifty, post-Revolution American take on the old European poultry pie — layers of chicken and butter under a rich crust, stripped of the medieval dates, gooseberries, and coxcombs in favor of "thrift and moderation." A New England Thanksgiving centerpiece before pumpkin pie took over.

Serves 8Prep 40 minBake 55 min

Ingredients

  • 3 lb chicken pieces, cooked and boned
  • 6 tbsp butter
  • 3 tbsp flour
  • 2 cups chicken broth
  • ½ tsp mace
  • ½ tsp pepper
  • 2 hard-boiled eggs, sliced (optional)
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Melt butter, whisk in flour, then broth, mace, and pepper; simmer into a light gravy.
  2. Layer chicken (and egg slices, if using) in a crust-lined deep dish; pour the gravy over.
  3. Top with a second crust, seal, and vent.
  4. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 50–55 minutes until deep gold. Rest 15 minutes before cutting.

Historical note: Colonial cooks deliberately pared back the sweet, elaborate European chicken pie to reflect post-Revolution values; it became a Thanksgiving essential, praised in Sarah Josepha Hale's 1827 novel Northwood.

recreated from 18th-century American poultry-pie recipes

Stargazy Pie

Savory · Cornwall, England

The most startling pie in Britain: whole pilchards or sardines baked with their heads poking up through the crust, "gazing at the stars." Traditionally made in the Cornish village of Mousehole around December 23 to honor a fisherman who braved a storm to feed the village.

Serves 6Prep 40 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 6 whole sardines or pilchards, cleaned
  • 4 eggs (2 hard-boiled, 2 beaten)
  • 4 oz bacon, chopped
  • 1 onion, chopped
  • ¾ cup cream or fish stock
  • 1 tbsp mustard
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Cook bacon and onion until soft; stir in cream and mustard, then cool.
  2. Line a pie dish with crust and add the bacon mixture and sliced hard-boiled egg.
  3. Arrange the sardines like spokes of a wheel, heads pointed outward to rest on the rim.
  4. Lay the top crust over the bodies, cutting slits so the heads poke through, gazing upward; brush with beaten egg.
  5. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 30–35 minutes until golden.

Historical note: Tied to the legend of Tom Bawcock of Mousehole, still celebrated on "Tom Bawcock's Eve" (December 23). The heads are left visible both for drama and so their rich oils drain back into the filling.

recreated from the traditional Mousehole, Cornwall dish

Medieval Coffyn Pye

Savory · England, c. 1390

Based on the "tartes of flesshe" recipe in The Forme of Cury: minced pork, hard-boiled egg, and cheese, colored gold with saffron. The original crust wasn't meant to be eaten — this version is.

Serves 6Prep 35 minBake 50 min

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground pork
  • 3 hard-boiled eggs, chopped
  • 1 cup shredded hard cheese
  • Pinch saffron threads, steeped in 1 tbsp warm water
  • 1 tsp ground ginger
  • ½ tsp black pepper
  • 2 double pie crusts (store-bought or homemade)
  • 1 egg, beaten, for sealing

Method

  1. Brown the pork fully, then drain and cool slightly.
  2. Mix pork with chopped eggs, cheese, saffron water, ginger, and pepper.
  3. Line a deep pie dish with crust, add filling, and top with a second crust; crimp and seal the edges with beaten egg.
  4. Cut a small steam vent in the top and bake at 375°F (190°C) for 45–50 minutes, until deep gold.
  5. Rest 15 minutes before cutting — the original coffyn was cracked open like a lid.

Historical note: The word "coffyn" simply meant box or basket. It became our word "coffin" for a burial box only later — the pastry, not the other way around.

recreated from The Forme of Cury, 1390

Roman Honey & Cheese Pie

Sweet · Rome, antiquity

A home version of the oldest known written pie recipe — a rye-crusted goat cheese pie sweetened with honey. Closer to a dense, rustic cheesecake than a modern pie.

Serves 8Prep 20 minBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups rye flour
  • ½ cup olive oil
  • ¼ cup cold water
  • 16 oz soft goat cheese
  • ½ cup honey, plus more to finish
  • 2 eggs
  • 1 tsp crushed bay leaf or dried mint

Method

  1. Combine rye flour, olive oil, and water into a rough dough; press into a pie dish.
  2. Beat goat cheese with honey, eggs, and bay leaf or mint until smooth.
  3. Pour filling into the crust and bake at 350°F (175°C) for 35–40 minutes, until just set.
  4. Cool, then drizzle generously with more honey before serving.

Historical note: Roman pies were commonly served cold and sliced like a loaf — try it chilled for the most authentic texture.

recreated from the earliest known written pie recipe

Colonial Pumpkin Pudding Pie

Sweet · England/America, 1675–1796

A simpler, less-sweet forerunner of the modern Thanksgiving pumpkin pie, closer to the version that appears in Amelia Simmons' 1796 "American Cookery."

Serves 8Prep 20 minBake 55 min

Ingredients

  • 2 cups cooked, mashed pumpkin or squash
  • 1 cup milk or cream
  • 3 eggs
  • ⅓ cup molasses
  • 1 tsp ginger
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • ¼ tsp salt
  • 1 single pie crust

Method

  1. Whisk pumpkin, milk, eggs, molasses, ginger, cinnamon, and salt until smooth.
  2. Pour into an unbaked crust.
  3. Bake at 375°F (190°C) for 15 minutes, then reduce to 325°F (165°C) and bake 35–40 minutes more, until set in the center.
  4. Cool fully before slicing.

Historical note: Pumpkin pie was not part of the 1621 harvest feast now remembered as the first Thanksgiving — it didn't become common in America until the early 1800s.

recreated from 17th–18th century English & American sources

Depression-era & desperation pies

6 recipes

"Desperation pies" made dessert out of almost nothing — eggs, flour, sugar, and whatever was in the pantry — when fruit, nuts, and money were all scarce. They stretch from the Civil War South through the Dust Bowl. (The Vinegar Pie and Mock Apple Pie further down are the two originals of the genre.)

Water Pie

Sweet · America, Great Depression

Exactly what it sounds like: a pie whose main ingredient is water. Born of the 1930s, it sets into a buttery, translucent custard that tastes something like pecan pie without the pecans — proof of how far a resourceful cook could stretch a pantry.

Serves 8Prep 10 minBake 55 min

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups water
  • 4 tbsp flour
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 5 tbsp butter, in pats
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 single pie crust, unbaked

Method

  1. Pour the water into the unbaked crust.
  2. Whisk flour and sugar together and sprinkle evenly over the water — do not stir.
  3. Drizzle vanilla over the top and dot with the butter pats.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 30 minutes, then reduce to 375°F (190°C) for 25 minutes more. It will look runny.
  5. Cool completely, then chill overnight — it sets into a firm, custardy pie with a thin buttery top layer.

Historical note: Water pie needs no eggs, milk, or fruit — the four cheap staples around the water are its entire luxury. It's one of the most literal expressions of Depression-era "make-do" baking.

recreated from 1930s American desperation recipes

Sugar Cream (Hoosier) Pie

Sweet · Indiana, early 1800s

Indiana's official state pie — an eggless custard of just cream, sugar, and flour, thought to have come from Quaker, Amish, or Shaker settlers. Sometimes called "finger pie," because the filling was traditionally stirred right in the shell with a finger.

Serves 8Prep 15 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 1 cup sugar
  • ⅓ cup cornstarch
  • 2¼ cups heavy cream
  • 4 tbsp butter
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 tsp cinnamon or nutmeg, to finish
  • 1 single pie crust, blind-baked

Method

  1. Whisk sugar and cornstarch in a saucepan, then whisk in cream.
  2. Cook over medium heat, stirring constantly, until thickened; stir in butter and vanilla.
  3. Pour into the blind-baked crust.
  4. Dust with cinnamon or nutmeg and bake at 375°F (190°C) for 10–15 minutes to set the top. Cool fully before slicing.

Historical note: A classic "make-do" pie for when the apple bins were empty, built entirely from what any dairy farm always had on hand. Named the official state pie of Indiana in 2009.

recreated from Indiana pioneer & Amish recipes

Shaker Lemon Pie

Sweet · Ohio Shakers, 18th century

A waste-nothing pie that uses the whole lemon — flesh, juice, peel, and pith — sliced paper-thin and macerated in sugar until jammy. Intensely tangy, and a lesson in Shaker frugality with a precious, expensive fruit.

Serves 8Prep 20 min + 4 hr macerateBake 40 min

Ingredients

  • 2 large lemons, sliced paper-thin (whole)
  • 2 cups sugar
  • 4 eggs, beaten
  • 3 tbsp flour
  • Pinch salt
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Toss the whole thin lemon slices with the sugar; cover and macerate at least 4 hours (overnight is better), until the peel softens and the mixture turns syrupy.
  2. Stir the beaten eggs, flour, and salt into the lemons.
  3. Pour into a crust-lined dish, arranging the slices evenly, and top with a second crust or a lattice; vent.
  4. Bake at 425°F (220°C) for 15 minutes, then reduce to 350°F (175°C) for 25 minutes more, until set and golden.

Historical note: Attributed to the Shaker communities of Ohio in the 1700s, where lemons were a rare luxury and using the entire fruit was a point of principle.

recreated from 18th-century Shaker recipes

Green Tomato Mock Apple Pie

Sweet · America, 19th–20th century

Another "mock apple" trick: firm green tomatoes, sliced and spiced, stand in for apples at the end of the season before the first frost — the acidity and lemon make the illusion surprisingly convincing.

Serves 8Prep 30 minBake 50 min

Ingredients

  • 5 cups green tomatoes, thinly sliced
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 3 tbsp flour
  • 2 tbsp lemon juice, plus zest
  • 1½ tsp cinnamon
  • ¼ tsp cloves
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Blanch the sliced green tomatoes in boiling water for 2 minutes, then drain well to soften and reduce bitterness.
  2. Toss with sugar, flour, lemon juice and zest, cinnamon, and cloves.
  3. Fill a crust-lined dish, dot with butter, and top with a lattice or vented crust.
  4. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 20 minutes, then reduce to 375°F (190°C) for 25–30 minutes more, until bubbling. Cool before slicing.

Historical note: A staple of thrifty gardeners racing the first frost — a way to turn an unripe crop that would otherwise be wasted into dessert.

recreated from American farm & Depression-era recipes

Desperation Vinegar Pie

Sweet · America, 19th century

A "desperation pie" built entirely from pantry staples for households without citrus — the vinegar mimics lemon's tang so convincingly it fooled generations of dinner guests.

Serves 8Prep 10 minBake 35 min

Ingredients

  • 1 cup sugar
  • 2 tbsp flour
  • 2 egg yolks
  • 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar
  • 1 cup water
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 1 single pie crust, blind-baked

Method

  1. Whisk sugar, flour, and egg yolks together in a saucepan.
  2. Stir in vinegar and water, then cook over medium heat, stirring constantly, until thick.
  3. Remove from heat and stir in butter until melted.
  4. Pour into the blind-baked crust and bake at 350°F (175°C) for 10 minutes to set the top.
  5. Chill at least 2 hours before serving.

Historical note: Desperation pies like this one, along with sugar cream pie and Kentucky transparent pie, were built to "stretch" scarce ingredients during hard times.

recreated from 19th-century American pantry recipes

Mock Apple (Cracker) Pie

Sweet · America, 1800s–1930s

No apples required. Sugar, cinnamon, and lemon transform plain crackers into a filling that — famously — tastes remarkably like apple pie.

Serves 8Prep 15 minBake 30 min

Ingredients

  • 36 plain crackers, broken
  • 1¾ cups water
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 2 tsp cream of tartar
  • 2 tbsp lemon juice, plus zest of 1 lemon
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 2 double pie crusts

Method

  1. Boil water, sugar, and cream of tartar for 5 minutes to make a syrup.
  2. Layer crackers in a crust-lined dish; pour the hot syrup over them and let sit 15 minutes to soften.
  3. Sprinkle with lemon juice, zest, cinnamon, and butter.
  4. Top with a second crust, seal, and bake at 425°F (220°C) for 25–30 minutes until golden.

Historical note: Pioneers on wagon trains improvised this pie when fresh apples weren't available; Ritz Crackers later printed a version of the recipe on their box in the 1930s.

recreated from 19th-century pioneer & Depression-era sources
